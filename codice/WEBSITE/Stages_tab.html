  • Passo della futa → Firenze

    PATH DETAILS

    • KM 70
    • Time 14 hours
    • Pacing 5 Km/h
    • Terrain An average gradient of 5% with some steeper sections reaching up to 10%.

    FOOD & WINE

    Florence is known for its delicious Tuscan cuisine, which includes dishes like bistecca alla fiorentina (a giant grilled T-bone steak), ribollita (a hearty vegetable soup), and crostini with various toppings like liver pâté or mushrooms. For a snack or dessert, runners can try cantucci, a type of almond biscotti that is often dipped in Vin Santo, a sweet dessert wine.

    HISTORY

    The Medici family were wealthy merchants who rose to power in the 15th century, becoming the most powerful family in Florence and eventually ruling over the entire region of Tuscany. The Medici family were patrons of the arts and sciences, and their patronage played a significant role in the development of the Renaissance in Italy. They sponsored artists such as Michelangelo, Leonardo da Vinci, and Botticelli, and their influence can still be seen throughout the city in the many museums and galleries dedicated to Renaissance art. One cool historical event that took place in the area around Florence is the Battle of Anghiari, which occurred in 1440 during a conflict between the city-states of Florence and Milan. The battle took place near the town of Anghiari and resulted in a victory for the Florentine army. The battle is notable for the depiction of its events in a famous fresco painting by Leonardo da Vinci, which was unfortunately lost over time. However, the search for the lost fresco continues to this day, with ongoing efforts to locate and restore the work of art.

    NICE TO KNOW

    • Firenze: Savonarola was a Dominican friar who gained great influence in Florence during the late 15th century. He preached against the moral corruption and decadence of the city's rulers and clergy, calling for a return to strict adherence to religious values. His powerful sermons attracted a large following and he eventually became the de facto ruler of Florence. He established a theocratic government and instituted strict laws regulating dress, behavior, and even art, which he deemed as immoral. However, Savonarola's reign was short-lived. His strict rule and fiery sermons earned him many enemies, both within and outside the city. In 1497, he was excommunicated by the Pope and later arrested by the ruling Medici family. He was tortured and eventually executed by hanging and burning in 1498. Savonarola's legacy remains controversial to this day. While some see him as a champion of morality and social justice, others view him as a religious fanatic and authoritarian ruler who stifled artistic and intellectual expression in Florence. His life and death have inspired many works of art and literature, including George Eliot's historical novel "Romola" and the play "The Tragedy of Savonarola" by Alfred de Vigny.

    HISTORY

    Bolsena is home to one of the most significant Eucharistic miracles in history. According to legend, in 1263, a traveling priest doubted the Catholic doctrine of transubstantiation, where the bread and wine become the actual body and blood of Jesus Christ during the Mass. As he celebrated Mass in Bolsena, the bread began to bleed, proving the doctrine to be true. The Miracle of Bolsena led to the institution of the Feast of Corpus Christi, and the town is now a pilgrimage site for Catholics.
